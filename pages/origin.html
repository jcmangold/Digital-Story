The Joker's Origin Story Explained

By Jacob M.
Aug. 28 2022

Should Joker have an Identity?

The Joker, Batman’s arch nemesis, Gotham City’s psychopathic villain, the GCPD’s relentless nightmare; The man who infects his victims with a gas that forces uncontrollable laughter until one dies with a smile plastered permanently on their face, his genius schemes put the rest of Batman’s Rogues Gallery to shame. But where did this maniac come from and how did he become so messed up?

Although the Joker originally appeared in the first issue of Batman, published in 1940, his origin wasn’t explored until issue 168, a full eleven years after the Joker’s first appearance. In this issue, Bill Finger, the main Batman writer at the time, revealed that the Joker had once been a criminal known as Red Hood. During a heated encounter with Batman, Red Hood stumbled into a vat of chemicals that gave him his infamous green hair and white skin, turning him into the Joker. Later, in 1988, writer Alan Moore took inspiration from this story when he wrote the well-known novel, The Killing Joke, which is now widely accepted as the Joker’s true origin.

The story begins like most older Batman and Joker comics end, with the Joker locked up in Arkham Asylum. Batman visits the Joker’s cell and tries to reason with the clown, telling him that eventually, one of them is going to die. Batman felt the need to know that he tried at least once to reason with the Joker and save countless lives. But something felt off to the detective. Joker hadn’t spoken a word throughout the entire encounter; he simply continued to deal out a set of playing cards. Frustrated, Batman grabbed the Joker’s hand as it placed down yet another card and exclaimed, “[a]re you even listening to me? It’s life and death that I’m discussing here” (Moore 10). Noticing the white smear on his palm, Batman suddenly realized what was wrong and lunged for the man in front of him, demanding to know what happened to the real Joker.

While Batman was wasting his time at the asylum, the Joker was preparing for his next big scheme: proving that all it took to turn a person insane was, “one bad day”; And his test subject was Jim Gordon, GCPD’s commissioner and longtime supporter of Batman(Moore 44). It’s just as Joker told Batman later in the book, “when I saw what a black, awful joke the world was, I went crazy as a coot” (Moore 45).As the Joker executes his master plan of torturing Jim Gordon, we are shown flashbacks of the man the Joker used to be before he went mad. We see that the Joker had quit his job at ACE Chemicals to become a comedian. But it wasn’t going so well. He wasn’t getting many job opportunities and the rent was due. On top of this, the Joker was trying to support his wife and unborn child. In desperation, he agreed to help break into ACE Chemical Labs with two other men. They gave him a red helmet, saying that if he wore it, no one would know who he was, mistaking him for a criminal known as the Red Hood. It would just be this once, he told himself, and then he could start a “proper” life with his wife and child (Moore 22).

Unfortunately, he would never get that chance. As the Joker remembers it, he was approached by an officer and informed that his wife and unborn child had passed away in an electrical accident. Distraught, the Joker hurried back to the two men he made a deal with and pleaded with them to let him back out of their deal. However, the men weren’t going to let him go so easily. They even threatened to kill the Joker if he tried to back out now.

Consequently, the Joker was forced to go along with the robbery. However, the three men were spotted by a guard during the break-in and the two crooks were shot. As the Joker fled for his life and the guards pursued, about to open fire again, Batman arrived and told the guards there would be no more shooting because he would handle the rest. In a desperate attempt to escape Batman, the Joker threw himself into a large vat of chemicals. He was spit out through a drainage tunnel and washed up on a bank. Taking off his helmet, the Joker took in his green hair and white skin reflecting at him from a puddle. Slowly, his red lips formed a hideous smile and he broke into uncontrollable laughter. The Joker had been born.

Here is the scene from the movie adaptation of the Killing Joke that illustrates the Joker's origin. In this clip we see batman chase a man through Ace Chemical Labs and watch as he falls into the chemicals and transform into the Joker. It is here when the man sees himself in a puddle that we first heae the Joker's iconic laugh.

But wait, the Joker is mentally insane. And we're basing this origin off of his memories. Can we even trust these memories? It’s like Joker said when Batman was chasing him through a carnival in the Killing Joke: “I’m not exactly sure. Sometimes I remember it one way, sometimes another” (Moore 45). So what do you think? Is this the Joker’s origin? Or did he just make it up? Do you like how the writers left the Joker's story open? Or do would you have preferred a more concrete origin? In any case, it’s genius how the writers give us an origin for one of DC’s most mysterious villains and yet at the same time leave it uncertain and open for debate. So maybe we don’t truly know the Joker’s origin, and though we can certainly come up with wild theories, this is definitely the closest we’ve gotten to an origin story for the Joker so far.
